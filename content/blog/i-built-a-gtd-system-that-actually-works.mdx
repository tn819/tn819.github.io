# I Built a GTD System That Actually Works

I spent three days setting up a GTD system in Obsidian. Here's what I learned.

Most productivity systems fail because they're too complicated. You spend more time managing the system than doing the work. I wanted something simple. Something that would get out of the way.

I think I got close.

## The Problem With Most GTD Setups

I've tried Todoist. I've tried Notion. I've tried paper notebooks. They all had the same flaw: they made me think about the system instead of the work.

Todoist was great until I had 47 overdue tasks and couldn't face opening it. Notion became a graveyard of beautifully organized plans I never executed. Paper was nice but I lost it.

What I needed was something that would:
1. Capture everything in under 5 seconds
2. Show me only what matters today
3. Force me to review weekly (or the system dies)
4. Archive ruthlessly

Obsidian with the right plugins does this. But it took some work to get there.

## What I Built

The system uses PARA (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archive) inside GTD workflows.

**Projects** are things with end dates. "Launch feature X" or "File taxes." Each gets one note in `/100 Projects/`. One note. One next action. If you open a project and can't see what to do next, the system is broken.

**Areas** are ongoing responsibilities. Health. Finances. Career. These never end, so they don't get checkboxes. They get standards. "Exercise 3x per week" is a standard I review in my Weekly Review. It's not a daily task that clutters my list.

This distinction matters. A lot.

**Capture happens three ways:**
- QuickAdd hotkey (`Cmd+Shift+A`) for desktop tasks
- Telegram bot on my phone for links, files, thoughts
- Web Clipper in the browser for full articles

Everything lands in one of three places: Inbox, Articles, or Resources. I process during Weekly Review. Not before.

## The Setup

It took about 3.5 hours spread over three days.

Day one was installing Obsidian and plugins. Tasks, Dataview, Periodic Notes, QuickAdd, Calendar, Telegram Sync. The usual suspects.

Day two was the painful part: creating all the templates and folder structure. Daily Notes, Weekly Reviews, Project templates, Area templates. 

## The Key Insight

Here's what nobody tells you: **Your system will break in two weeks if you don't do Weekly Review.**

I skipped my first Weekly Review. By day 10, I had 23 items in my Inbox and I stopped capturing because "I'll process it later." The system died.

I restarted. Did Weekly Review religiously. The system works.

The Weekly Review takes 45-60 minutes. I do it Sunday evening with coffee. Same time every week. Non-negotiable.

During review, I:
1. Process Inbox to zero
2. Review every active project (does it have a next action?)
3. Review Areas (am I meeting my standards?)
4. Review Waiting For (anything I need to follow up on?)
5. Brain dump anything not captured

If you do nothing else, do Weekly Review.

## What Works

**The Inbox as sacred space.** Nothing stays there more than 48 hours. Ever. It's not storage. It's triage.

**One next action per project.** Not a list of 12 steps. One thing. The very next physical action. When that's done, you figure out the next one.

**Areas as standards.** This was the game-changer. I used to have "Work out" as a recurring daily task. Failed constantly. Now I have a "Fitness Standard" document I review weekly. It says "Exercise 3x per week, minimum 30 minutes." No daily guilt. Just weekly accountability.

**Archiving ruthlessly.** If I haven't touched a project in two weeks and it's not urgent, it goes to Archive. Not deleted. Just out of sight. I can resurrect it later. This keeps my active project list under 10 items.

## What Doesn't Work

**Trying to capture and process at the same time.** These are different mental modes. Capture is fast and dumb. Process is slow and thoughtful. I used to try to "quickly categorize" things when capturing. Never worked. Now everything goes to Inbox, period.

**More than 7 active projects.** I don't know why 7 is the magic number, but it is. More than that and I can't hold them all in my head. Everything stalls.

**Skipping Weekly Review.** Already mentioned but worth repeating. Skip it once, you can recover. Skip it twice, the system dies.

**Perfectionism.** Your folder structure doesn't matter. Your template formatting doesn't matter. What matters is: Do you trust the system? Do you actually use it? Optimize for trust, not aesthetics.

## The Results

Three months in:

- **Zero items in my head.** Everything is captured. My mind is clear during focused work.
- **Weekly Review completion rate: 100%.** Because I know what happens if I skip.
- **Projects completed: 12.** Versus probably 3 in the same period before.
- **Anxiety about forgetting things: Gone.** The system has it.

The biggest change: I can be present. When I'm working, I'm working. When I'm with people, I'm with people. I'm not mentally tracking to-dos because I know the system has them.

## Build Your Own

You don't need to copy my exact setup. The principles matter more than the tools:

1. **Capture everything** in one place
2. **Process regularly** (Weekly Review minimum)
3. **One next action** per project
4. **Archive ruthlessly**
5. **Trust the system** (no mental backup lists)

Use Obsidian, use Notion, use paper. Whatever you'll actually use.
